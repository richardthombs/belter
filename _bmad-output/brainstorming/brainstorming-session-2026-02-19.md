## Technique Execution Results

### Technique 1: What If Scenarios

**[Physics #1]: Accretion as Gameplay**
_Concept:_ Small asteroids gravitationally drift toward larger ones over time, slowly increasing mass and resource density. A "seed" asteroid left alone long enough becomes a mega-body worth serious effort to mine.
_Novelty:_ The world keeps generating value passively — players who scout early and revisit later find richer targets than they left behind.

**[Physics #2]: Composition-Gated Collision Outcomes**
_Concept:_ Collision results are deterministic based on material composition + kinetic energy. Iron-on-iron at high speed fragments. Ice-on-rock at low speed accretes. Players who understand the physics can predict and exploit outcomes.
_Novelty:_ The physics becomes a skill layer. A smart player doesn't just mine — they engineer collisions to produce the asteroid type they want.

**[Physics #3]: Vaporisation Events**
_Concept:_ Catastrophic high-energy collisions produce a diffuse resource cloud — a temporary, dispersing field of raw material that any nearby player can scoop. Gone in minutes if uncollected.
_Novelty:_ Creates spontaneous high-tension PvE moments — a massive collision just happened somewhere on the map, everyone races to harvest before it dissipates.

**[Physics #4]: Asteroid Steering Infrastructure**
_Concept:_ To redirect an asteroid, players must physically attach thruster arrays or mass drivers to its surface — hardware that requires rare materials to build, time to install, and ongoing fuel to operate. Larger asteroids need more units, potentially requiring organised multi-player cooperation.
_Novelty:_ The capability is real but genuinely expensive — it's a late-game, high-investment play rather than something any solo player can do casually.

**[Intel #1]: Trajectory Knowledge as Perishable Asset**
_Concept:_ Asteroid trajectory data has a timestamp. The moment you acquire it, a clock starts ticking on its validity. Any collision, steering event, or gravitational interaction since that timestamp may have invalidated it — and you won't know unless you have a live feed.
_Novelty:_ Knowledge isn't just valuable, it expires. This fundamentally changes how information is priced and traded — fresh data commands premiums, old data is cheap but risky.

**[Intel #2]: The Ghost Asteroid**
_Concept:_ A player navigates to coordinates where a rich asteroid was surveyed three days ago. Nothing there. No warning, no notification — just empty space. They have to deduce what happened: collision? Steering? Did someone get there first and strip it?
_Novelty:_ Creates genuine mystery and tension from the absence of information, not its presence. The empty space tells a story.

**[Intel #3]: Radio Beacons as Living Intelligence Infrastructure**
_Concept:_ Attaching a beacon to an asteroid converts static snapshot knowledge into a live data stream — continuously broadcasting updated position, velocity, mass changes. The beacon is a physical object: findable, destroyable, takeable.
_Novelty:_ Intelligence infrastructure becomes a first-class strategic asset. Placing, protecting, hunting, and destroying beacons is its own profession.

**[Intel #4]: Obsolescence vs. Decay**
_Concept:_ Survey data never becomes false — it becomes a historical record. "This asteroid was at these coordinates with this composition on this date." Whether that's still true depends on what happened since. Old data is cheap but potentially still useful as a baseline; fresh data commands premium pricing.
_Novelty:_ Players make probabilistic judgments, not binary valid/invalid checks. A miner might gamble on week-old data for a low-value run, but invest in fresh surveys before committing serious capital.

**[Ship Design #1]: The Survey Specialist**
_Concept:_ A player who strips out cargo bays, weapons, and engine capacity to fit oversized sensor arrays and long-range scanners. They move slowly, carry little, fight poorly — but can read an asteroid's composition, mass, and trajectory vector from further away and with greater accuracy than anyone else in the sector.
_Novelty:_ Creates a pure intelligence-gathering role with real market value — their product is data, not ore.

**[Ship Design #2]: Sensor Trade-Off as Core Design Language**
_Concept:_ Every ship has a finite set of hardpoints and power budget. A survey array is large and power-hungry. Fitting one means fewer mining lasers, smaller cargo, weaker engines, or less armour. No ship can do everything well — specialisation is always a meaningful sacrifice.
_Novelty:_ Player identity emerges from build decisions, not just progression. Two players at the same "level" can have radically different capabilities and market roles.

**[Economy #1]: The Survey Catalogue**
_Concept:_ A surveyor doesn't just sell individual data points — they sell access to a maintained regional catalogue. Subscribers pay a recurring fee for up-to-date composition maps. The surveyor's income is steady; the miner's efficiency is dramatically higher.
_Novelty:_ Creates a genuine service economy around information — and gives surveyors a business model beyond one-time data sales.

**[Profession #1]: The Surveyor**
_Concept:_ Dedicated scanning ships run survey passes through sectors, building trajectory databases they can sell. A surveyor who's mapped a sector thoroughly and recently is sitting on perishable but high-value inventory. They're not mining anything — they're selling the map.
_Novelty:_ Creates a pure intelligence profession with its own risk/reward loop — surveyors need to work fast and sell before data ages, or invest in beacons to keep data live.

**[Profession #2]: The Intelligence Broker**
_Concept:_ Mid-players who buy bulk trajectory data cheaply, package it, and resell it to miners and corporations at a markup — essentially running an information arbitrage business.
_Novelty:_ A non-combat, non-mining economic role that can be extremely lucrative and politically powerful.

**[Social #1]: The Witness Mechanic**
_Concept:_ Aggression against another player is only reportable if witnessed. Being seen committing an act — by another player whose ship was in sensor range — generates an evidence record that can be submitted to an aligned organisation. No witnesses? It didn't officially happen.
_Novelty:_ Creates genuine tension around visibility. Attackers want empty space; victims want busy sectors. Smart criminals pick their moments carefully.

**[Social #2]: Organisational Alignment & Standing**
_Concept:_ Players align with organisations — some NPC factions, some player-run guilds with recognised authority. Standing with each organisation is tracked separately. High standing unlocks docking rights, trade discounts, tech access, and protected space. Low standing means hostile territory, denied services, bounties.
_Novelty:_ Political identity has real mechanical consequences. Who you've worked for and against shapes where you can safely operate.

**[Social #3]: The Sheriff Profession**
_Concept:_ Players who align deeply with a law-enforcement organisation gain formal authority within their jurisdiction — the right to pursue wanted players, execute arrest warrants, collect bounties. Being a sheriff isn't cosmetic — it grants access, bounty payments, and legal cover for acts that would make others wanted.
_Novelty:_ Emergent roleplay with mechanical teeth.

**[Economy #2]: Player-Made Ships as the Only Premium Ships**
_Concept:_ No NPC shipyard sells premium vessels. Ships exist because a player mined the ore, refined it, and either built it themselves or paid a shipbuilder to do it. Destroy a ship and those materials are genuinely gone from the economy.
_Novelty:_ Every ship has provenance. Supply and demand for ships fluctuates with the availability of rare materials. A skilled shipbuilder is as valuable as a skilled miner.

**[Economy #3]: Material Tiers Drive Ship Capability**
_Concept:_ Common iron-nickel asteroids produce basic hulls. Rare compositions — exotic alloys, carbonaceous deposits, crystalline structures — unlock superior ship components. A ship built from rare materials genuinely outperforms one built from common stock.
_Novelty:_ The asteroid belt's composition diversity directly maps to a technology tree. Rare asteroid hunters are effectively unlocking the game's best content.

**[Property #1]: Asteroid Claiming**
_Concept:_ Before building any infrastructure on an asteroid, a player must formally claim it — requiring a fee, a faction registration, or a physical claim beacon. Claiming grants resource ownership and building rights but also assigns liability for collisions involving that asteroid.
_Novelty:_ Ownership is meaningful and consequential, not cosmetic.

**[Property #2]: Unauthorised Mining as a Witnessed Offence**
_Concept:_ Mining on a claimed asteroid without the owner's permission is an infringement. If witnessed by a player or NPC with faction affiliation, the transgressor loses standing with the faction that registered the claim.
_Novelty:_ The witness mechanic applies consistently across all offences — one elegant rule covers combat, theft, and trespass alike.

**[Economy #4]: Basic Starter Ships as a Floor**
_Concept:_ A small set of NPC-sold basic hulls are always available at starter stations — cheap, capable enough to take contracts and mine common asteroids, but meaningfully inferior to player-crafted vessels. They're the safety net, not the aspiration.
_Novelty:_ New players are never stuck, but they're immediately motivated to graduate to player-economy ships.

**[Economy #5]: Mining Contracts**
_Concept:_ Asteroid owners post contracts specifying the resource, volume, delivery location, and payment. Any player with a mining ship can accept and fulfil them. The owner gets resources extracted without personal effort; the contractor earns income without needing their own claim.
_Novelty:_ Creates a thriving gig economy — new players on day one have immediate, meaningful work that feeds into the broader player economy.

**[Property #3]: Winner-Takes-All Accretion**
_Concept:_ When two claimed asteroids merge, ownership follows mass — the larger body's owner inherits the combined asteroid. The losing party's claim is simply extinguished. No arbitration, no compensation, no shared ownership.
_Novelty:_ Rewards investing in larger asteroids and introduces genuine risk to owning small ones in dense asteroid fields.

### Technique 2: Cross-Pollination

**[Economy #6]: The Crafting Supply Chain**
_Concept:_ Individual crafters buy raw materials, craft components or equipment, and sell them on open markets. A solo player who specialises in sensor array components can run a profitable one-person operation entirely within the player economy. Supply chain: Miner → raw ore → Refiner → materials → Crafter → components → Shipbuilder → ships → Everyone.
_Novelty:_ Crafting is a complete playstyle with no combat, no mining, no exploration required.

**[Economy #7]: Split Marketplace — Browse Global, Collect Local**
_Concept:_ Any player can list goods and browse/purchase from anywhere. Physical goods sit at the seller's location until the buyer travels to collect. Information, survey data, and blueprints are delivered instantly on purchase.
_Novelty:_ Geography still matters for physical goods without making the market opaque.

**[Economy #8]: Information as a First-Class Digital Asset**
_Concept:_ Survey data, trajectory records, composition catalogues, and blueprints occupy a separate asset class — purchasable and deliverable anywhere instantly.
_Novelty:_ Creates a genuinely frictionless information economy running parallel to the physical goods economy.

**[Profession #3]: The Hauler-Arbitrageur**
_Concept:_ A player sees cheap refined titanium listed in sector 3 and high demand in sector 11. They buy it, travel to collect, fly it to sector 11, and pocket the margin. Pure economic gameplay — spreadsheet skill and spatial awareness as the core loop.
_Novelty:_ No mining, no combat, no crafting required. Market reading and logistics as a viable career.

**[Infrastructure #1]: NPC Station Network**
_Concept:_ A distributed set of NPC-owned stations provide baseline services — refining, refuelling, ship upgrades, contract boards, basic ship sales — throughout governed space. Always accessible, never destroyable, charging market rates.
_Novelty:_ The floor that makes the game always playable, and the benchmark player-built stations must beat.

**[Infrastructure #2]: Player Organisation Stations on Large Asteroids**
_Concept:_ Player orgs construct modular stations on sufficiently large claimed asteroids. The asteroid itself is the foundation; lose the asteroid, lose the station. Buildable modules: Dockyard, Refuelling Point, Sensor Array, Beacon, Defences (vs ships and incoming asteroids).
_Novelty:_ Player infrastructure is genuinely at risk — from rivals, from physics, from poor claim management.

**[Infrastructure #3]: Station Gravity — The Player Hub Effect**
_Concept:_ A well-built player station with dockyard, refuelling, and sensors naturally attracts traffic, becoming the de facto hub for a region and giving its owners enormous economic and political leverage.
_Novelty:_ Player power comes from building something other players need to use. Infrastructure as influence.

**[Economy #9]: Dynamic NPC Price Competition**
_Concept:_ NPC stations continuously adjust their rates in response to player-station pricing in the same region. Player stations can beat NPC rates on convenience, speed, loyalty perks, and location — but can't exploit captive markets.
_Novelty:_ Creates healthy competition without developer intervention. The NPC network is the economic immune system.

**[Economy #10]: Location as Pricing Power**
_Concept:_ The further a player station is from the nearest NPC station, the more pricing latitude it has. Frontier stations can charge meaningful premiums; stations near NPC hubs must compete on value-adds.
_Novelty:_ Geography becomes economic strategy.

**[Death #1]: The Ghost Ship**
_Concept:_ When a ship is destroyed, the wreck remains at the destruction coordinates. The pilot respawns in a backup vessel. Only they know the exact location. They can race back to salvage it, sell the coordinates, or leave it.
_Novelty:_ Death has consequence and texture without being purely punishing. The wreck location is a new tradeable piece of intelligence.

**[Death #2]: Wreck Discovery by Others**
_Concept:_ A sufficiently equipped survey ship scanning a sector might detect a wreck signature. They don't know what's in it until they reach it. Finding someone else's wreck is a legitimate and exciting discovery event.
_Novelty:_ Creates opportunistic salvage gameplay. Wrecks are hidden treasure — some worthless, some carrying rare components.

**[Death #3]: The Fleet System**
_Concept:_ Players maintain a personal fleet of ships stored at stations. Losing your active ship sends you to your next stored vessel. Lose everything? The free starter ship is always waiting.
_Novelty:_ Progression feels genuinely meaningful — your fleet is your wealth. But the floor ensures the game never becomes unplayable.

**[World #1]: Procedurally Generated Expanding Universe**
_Concept:_ The asteroid belt is a living, procedurally generated universe that expands outward as players push the frontier. New sectors open with fresh asteroid distributions, undiscovered compositions, and unclaimed territory. Inner regions are settled and political; the outer edge is wild, unknown, and rich.
_Novelty:_ There's always a frontier. The game never "completes." Perpetual gold rush dynamic at the edge of the known world.

**[World #2]: Multi-Server Scale**
_Concept:_ The universe is distributed across server infrastructure, with players seamlessly crossing boundaries as they travel. Distance from civilisation is genuinely meaningful — a player in the deep frontier is operating alone in a way an inner-belt player never experiences.
_Novelty:_ Scale itself becomes a game mechanic.

**[Gameplay #1]: Assisted Newtonian Flight Model**
_Concept:_ Ships move with genuine physics momentum. Retro thrusters fire automatically on key release, counteracting rotation and drift at a rate determined by thruster power vs ship mass. A light scout stops almost instantly; a heavy mining barge takes noticeably longer.
_Novelty:_ Flight feel is intrinsically tied to ship build. Loadout speaks through the controls.

**[Gameplay #2]: Thruster as a Meaningful Component**
_Concept:_ Rotational, retro, and main thrusters are separate craftable/purchasable components with power and efficiency ratings. Better thrusters don't show up as a number — they show up as a ship that responds the way you want it to.
_Novelty:_ Upgrades feel good in your hands, not just on a stat sheet.

### Technique 3: Concept Blending

**[Blend A]: The Cartographer's Gold Rush**
_Concept:_ The frontier is always opening. The first surveyor into a new sector owns the most perishable and valuable information in the game — composition catalogues, claim coordinates, beacon networks — before a single rock is mined. They sell the map before anyone sells the ore.
_Novelty:_ A perpetual first-mover economy at the frontier edge, where information is the first resource extracted from any new territory.

**[Blend D]: The Reach of Law**
_Concept:_ Governed space isn't defined by a line on a map — it's defined by sensor coverage. Where player stations have sensor arrays and beacon networks, witnesses exist and law functions. Player organisations extend jurisdiction physically, outpost by outpost.
_Novelty:_ Law as a physical, expandable infrastructure layer. The frontier recedes as civilisation advances, sensor tower by sensor tower.

**[Blend A+D]: The Cartographer-Sheriff Alliance**
_Concept:_ Surveyor organisations and law-enforcement factions form natural alliances. Surveyors push into new frontier sectors planting beacons that serve both commercial and jurisdictional purposes simultaneously. The cartographer is the vanguard of civilisation — accidentally building the infrastructure of law as they build the infrastructure of commerce.
_Novelty:_ Two completely different player archetypes discover they need each other. Emergent political alliance from mechanical design, not developer scripting.

## Design Decisions

- **Asteroid steering** (v1 scope): Thruster attachments exist for gentle nudging but precision collision plotting is deferred. Liability: if your thrusters are on it, it's your responsibility.
- **Information obsolescence**: Data doesn't decay — it becomes a historical record. Whether it reflects current reality depends on what happened since capture.
- **Ship destruction frequency**: Rare and significant in MMO context. Wrecks exist but salvage hunting is opportunistic emergent gameplay, not a designed profession.
- **Claim collision rule**: Winner-takes-all by mass. Largest surviving body inherits the original claim.
- **Marketplace**: Global browse and purchase; physical collection requires travel; information delivered instantly.
- **Flight model**: Assisted Newtonian — retro thrusters auto-compensate on key release, rate governed by thruster efficiency vs ship mass.
- **New player floor**: Free starter ship always available if all owned ships are lost.

## Design Principles

**[Design Principle #1]: Emergent Gameplay Over Prescribed Loops**
Don't design specific careers — design systems that interact. The wreck hunter, the intelligence broker, the claim speculator — none need explicit support. They fall out naturally from underlying mechanics. The best gameplay loops are the ones the developers didn't plan.

## Core Identity

**A game whose soul is the expansion of civilisation into a procedurally infinite frontier**, where every profession — surveyor, miner, builder, sheriff, hauler, crafter — plays a distinct role in that expansion. Inner space is settled, political, and mapped. The frontier is wild, unknown, and rich. The tension between safety and opportunity is the game's beating heart.
